Useless and Uselessness

Posted on 2022-05-15 Edited on 2024-08-23 In Times
Symbols count in article: 444 Reading time ≈ 2 mins.

“Dao”

This weekend, I revisited the TV series The Dao and came across a profound discussion about the concept of “Dao”:

———

Ding Yuanying said: “There are three levels to understanding society: technology, systems, and culture. Whether for an individual or a nation, any fate ultimately stems from cultural attributes. Dominant cultures create strong individuals, while weak cultures produce weak ones. This is a rule, and it can be understood as **the Dao, which is not subject to human will.**”

“Dominant cultures follow the laws of things, while weak cultures rely on the moral expectations of the strong, seeking salvation. Dominant cultures are like ‘secret manuals’ in martial arts, while weak cultures, being easier to learn, understand, and use, become popular.”

Rui Xiaodan asked: “I can understand this from the literal sense, but how does it apply?”

Ding Yuanying replied: “**Useless and Uselessness.**”

———

The phrase “Useless and Uselessness” precisely addresses the answer to my recent intellectual dilemma.

The term “Dao” represents the principles I have long sought, things that are not subject to personal will, which could be laws, ways of thinking, or various forms.

Through prolonged exploration, I have come to believe in the foundational role of Formal Logic and the worldview of Marxist Philosophy.

Even though I have established logic and Marxist philosophy as foundational driving forces, I still found myself confused in practical application. Perhaps this is because the process of applying these theories in practice often brings new insights.

I have questioned how logic and philosophy can be applied in real life. Rui Xiaodan’s question, “What’s the use?” highlights this concern.

Useless might seem to have no direct application, but Uselessness aligns with a belief I have long held, although I had not fully committed to it before.

The boundary of Uselessness lies in the fact that logic and philosophy can indeed be applied to various aspects of daily life and work.

I feel fortunate to have gained some insight in my 30s. This insight has provided a clear answer from the “Dao”. The answer has been within me all along, only needing a reason to believe and persist.

An interesting question is: Humans often seek meaning in their actions, but is this meaning truly important?

I’m still not sure, but for many things, it’s crucial for me to understand “why”.

The concept of “Useless and Uselessness” embodies the principle of simplicity. What seems simple is actually challenging to achieve, while complex things can be simplified, often due to our misunderstanding or overcomplication of the unknown.

Laozi said: “Wu wei but nothing is not done.”

Following the “Dao”, seeking truth from facts, and observing the cause and effect in our actions.
