What You Focus on Echoes Back

Posted on 2022-05-07 Edited on 2024-08-27 In Times Word count in article: 370 Reading time ≈ 1 mins.
Explore the philosophical concept of rebirth and how our focus and perceptions shape our understanding of the world and ourselves.

Rebirth

A classic philosophical paradox is Theseus’ Ship: If the wooden planks of Theseus’ ship are gradually replaced until none of the original planks remain, is it still the same ship?

Some say yes, others say no. If our cells regenerate daily, and eventually, all of our cells are replaced, are we still the same person? And if our thoughts and perceptions evolve every day, are we still the same self?

Perhaps the answer doesn’t matter. What truly matters is that life continues, and new beginnings emerge.

Material conditions shape consciousness, but consciousness also impacts material reality. A new understanding can lead to a form of rebirth.

In English, the prefix “re” signifies repetition or renewal, as seen in words like reborn, rework, rebuild, review, restart, and recall.

In the digital age, technological advancements continuously reinvent industries. From web1.0 to web2.0, and now the emerging web3.0, each phase has marked a rebirth of various sectors.

For individuals, many of our perceptions are shaped by our upbringing and environment. However, these perceptions aren’t always accurate. Human behavior is driven by habits, which in turn are shaped by consciousness. Therefore, should we not re-examine our past knowledge, concepts, and beliefs?

This might be a lengthy journey, but certain key ideas are worth reassessing. Over the past few years, I have sought the root of specific issues, recently finding answers in two textbooks: Formal Logic and Marxist Philosophy. Perhaps my college exposure to these subjects has led me to this path of introspection.

In essence, it’s a re-evaluation of the Three Views: worldview, values, and outlook on life.

Marxist philosophy posits that the relationship between humans and the world centers around two main activities: understanding the world and transforming it. All human activities are focused on these two goals. This also explains why “carving oneself and benefiting others” is a fundamental drive and value. First, we must understand ourselves and the world, and then we transform it.

Everyone has the opportunity for rebirth, but it requires a re-examination of oneself and the world—a baptism of the Three Views.

In my value system, only through a deep understanding of oneself and the world can we find a peaceful path forward.
