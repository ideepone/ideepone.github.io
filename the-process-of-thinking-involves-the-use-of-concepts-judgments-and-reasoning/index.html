The Process of Thinking Involves the Use of Concepts, Judgments, and Reasoning

We often discuss “logic,” but what exactly does it mean?

The term “logic” originates from the Greek word λογος (logos), which means thought, reason, principle, word, etc. In everyday language, “logic” can be understood in three distinct ways:

1. The Regularity of Interconnection: It refers to the regularity in the interconnection and development of objective things and the regularity of human thinking that reflects this. For example, “the logic of the Chinese revolution” or “the logic of things.” Here, “logic” pertains to the regularity of objective phenomena. Similarly, “drawing a logical conclusion” or describing an article as having strong logic refers to the regularity in thinking.

2. A Theory, Viewpoint, or Method: It represents a specific theory, viewpoint, or method of reasoning. For instance, when invaders label their aggression as “friendship,” we criticize it as “pure robber logic.” In this context, “logic” refers to the distorted reasoning or method of understanding.

3. The Science of Correct Thinking: It denotes the study of the forms and laws of correct thinking, including formal logic and dialectical logic, though commonly it refers to formal logic. For example, “learning grammar and logic” or “high school students studying logic.” Here, “logic” signifies the science of formal logic.

What is Formal Logic?

Formal logic studies the forms and laws of thinking and is primarily a science of thinking. Engels described logic as the doctrine of the laws governing the thinking process.

Understanding Thinking

Mao Zedong, in his “On Practice,” described the cognitive process: People initially perceive only the phenomena of things, their one-sidedness, and external connections. This represents the perceptual stage of cognition, where profound concepts or logical conclusions are not yet formed. As social practice continues, repeated experiences lead to a transformation in cognition, resulting in concept formation.

Recently, I found this explanation of the origin of “concepts” particularly insightful.

The thinking process is an abstract and indirect reflection of the objective world, characterized by abstraction and indirectness. This reflection is mediated through language.

The process of thinking involves concepts, judgments, and reasoning—essential forms of thinking used to reflect objective reality, which logic refers to as forms of thought.

The content above is adapted from the book Formal Logic, compiled by the Department of Philosophy at East China Normal University. The book is now in its fifth edition, having significantly improved from its first edition drafted in 1963.

My interest in this book stems from a desire to understand the world accurately. Formal logic addresses the formal laws of thinking, regardless of content. This aligns with my exploration of whether a fixed paradigm exists for reflecting the objective world subjectively. To me, logic encompasses three core elements: concepts, judgments, and reasoning.

While science may not always be perfect, it currently represents the worldview I adhere to. I believe that the Chinese education system could benefit from a broader emphasis on logic. In the future, I might even advocate for the importance of logic in education.

Ultimately, understanding the world involves grasping the dialectical relationship between oneself and the world—a reciprocal, spiraling journey from the mundane to the profound, interacting between consciousness and matter.
