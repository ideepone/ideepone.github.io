Simple

Posted on 2023-08-26 Edited on 2024-08-12
Symbols count in article: 422 Reading time ≈ 2 mins.

Many years ago, I might have been someone who delved into various fields of knowledge, and perhaps I still am to this day, but now I’m captivated by a very simple principle: “Keep it simple.”

This principle, originally known as “K.I.S.S” - Keep it simple, stupid.

Albert Einstein once said:

“Everything should be made as simple as possible, but no simpler.”

“It can scarcely be denied that the supreme goal of all theory is to make the irreducible basic elements as simple and as few as possible without having to surrender the adequate representation of a single datum of experience.”

For many things, we often think they are too complex, too complex to understand. In fact, the world can be broken down into simplicity. Zhang Chaoyang once shared this insight.

I recall Fu Peirong discussing Western philosophy in class, mentioning one of Heraclitus’ thoughts: “All things come from fire.”

This perspective may seem erroneous now, but at that time, I found it impressive. He was seeking the most fundamental construction of the world and drew a conclusion.

Perhaps this is one of the origins of materialism, and it’s quite significant.

When discussing logic, we also need to clarify the concept first, as the concept is the most basic unit of semantic understanding.

Back during my student days, when I first encountered chemical molecules and atoms, one day our chemistry teacher told us that both diamond and charcoal are composed of carbon elements. However, diamond is C60, and charcoal is C4, just differing in structural bonds.

In the workplace, we often break down tasks in planning, aiming to simplify tasks to the utmost, to the point where they are crystal clear.

The periodic table of elements is a marvelous thing, and it remains so to this day. Bill Gates has an element wall in his office, reportedly collecting physical samples of all elements except radioactive ones.

I’ve also attempted to understand the simplicity of this world, trying to peel away the layers of its various exteriors.

The world originally might have consisted of individual entities composed of these elements, and then due to various connections, the world gained its rich diversity, forming its shape.

More than 90% of things in this world can be broken down to a simple level of comprehension. If they cannot, it only suggests that we are still unable to understand, unable to delve into the truth and essence, requiring continual exploration.

The world of computers has only 0 and 1. Is it possible that our world is the same?

Assuming the World Is Comprehensible, Where Lies Its Comprehensibility?

Posted on 2023-01-06 Edited on 2024-08-15 In Times
Symbols count in article: 472 Reading time ≈ 2 mins.

Comprehension

The most incomprehensible thing about the world is that it is comprehensible.
— Albert Einstein

What exactly was Albert Einstein’s understanding of 「comprehension」 regarding this world?

Was it quantum mechanics, probabilistic randomness, or something else? Einstein might not have originally said this quote; it could have been beautified by the media. But where exactly does this 「domain」 of “comprehension” lie?

He said, “God does not play dice.”

But the behavior of microscopic particles is determined by probability. Randomness and probability are the norms of this world.

The macroscopic world is no different. Behind countless decisions and actions lie various complex events and conditions influencing the outcome.

Socrates

All men are mortal;

Socrates is a man;

Therefore, Socrates is mortal.

What if probability was suddenly added behind inductive and deductive logic?

99% of all men are mortal;

Socrates has an 80% chance of being a man;

So, is Socrates mortal? What is the likelihood of his mortality?

Randomness Is Not the Same as Uncertainty

The biggest difference between randomness and uncertainty lies in whether the possible outcomes of an event are known.

Randomness means that I know all the possible outcomes of an event, but I don’t know which outcome will occur next.

I once thought this was process thinking, meaning that when you’ve experienced something, you can clearly see the entire process of its development. By looking through the river of history or even understanding it from a God’s-eye view, many things seem natural.

But unfortunately, it doesn’t seem to be that way. For instance, why do different ideologies and camps exist? Even our Earth might only be a random existence.

Our expectations for the future and our attempts to understand future uncertainties hinge on continuously transforming uncertainties into randomness, because most things in this world are random, not uncertain.

God might enjoy playing dice, but we’re fortunate if we know that the dice only have numbers 1 through 6. However, if we don’t know whether the numbers on God’s dice might exceed 6, that’s uncertainty, and it even sounds a bit terrifying.

The Endpoint of Probability Is 1

Probability has three properties:

First, probability is always between 0 and 1; it can never be negative.

Second, the sum of the probabilities of all basic events in the sample space is 1.

Third, the probability of a random event not occurring is equal to 1 minus the probability of it occurring.

The second property is particularly interesting: the sum of probabilities is 1, or 100%. It seems obvious, but this “1” has two meanings.

First, our sample must be complete, without omissions, which is difficult to achieve in reality.

Second, probability is a necessary event under a system, but a contingent event under details.

To make this event as likely as possible to occur, one must delve deeply into understanding it.

Many Times, What We Say is Just a Cliché

Posted on 2022-05-28 Edited on 2024-08-23 In Times
Symbols count in article: 298 Reading time ≈ 1 mins.

Seek Truth from Facts

If a phrase is repeated over and over until no one knows what it means anymore, it may become just a cliché.

Back in high school, we had a debate. During the argument, both sides kept mentioning worldview. Finally, one of our opponents asked, “We keep talking about ‘worldview’, but can your side explain what exactly is a worldview?” We were stunned and couldn’t answer. The entire class was present, and even our teacher, who had been quietly listening, applauded when this question was asked.

This debate left a deep impression on me, especially regarding the question: What is a worldview? Another realization was that many concepts we think we understand well and can easily talk about are often just clichés, repeated without deep thought.

What Does “Seek Truth from Facts” Mean?

The Book of Han records: “Study diligently, love antiquity, and seek truth from facts.”

Mao Zedong also explained “seeking truth from facts”: “Facts” refer to all objectively existing things. “Truth” refers to the internal connections of these objective things, that is, their regularity. “Seek” means we study them.”

Often, when we approach and solve problems, we rely on subjective perceptions, only on experience, without investigation, and sometimes based on unfounded sources.

What are facts, what are opinions, what is objective, and what is subjective? Only by basing our analysis on these objective facts can we rationally analyze and solve problems. The foundation of critical thinking is based on facts. We often talk about the madness of crowds and collective wisdom, but what matters is whether the conclusions drawn are based on facts or on opinions.

What kind of world do you understand? What kind of world do you believe in? Try to clarify these slowly, and many problems will naturally be resolved.

“Dao”

———

Ding Yuanying said: “There are three levels to understanding society: technology, systems, and culture. Whether for an individual or a nation, any fate ultimately stems from cultural attributes. Dominant cultures create strong individuals, while weak cultures produce weak ones. This is a rule, and it can be understood as **the Dao, which is not subject to human will.**”

Rui Xiaodan asked: “I can understand this from the literal sense, but how does it apply?”

Ding Yuanying replied: “**Useless and Uselessness.**”

———

The phrase “Useless and Uselessness” precisely addresses the answer to my recent intellectual dilemma.

The term “Dao” represents the principles I have long sought, things that are not subject to personal will, which could be laws, ways of thinking, or various forms.

Through prolonged exploration, I have come to believe in the foundational role of Formal Logic and the worldview of Marxist Philosophy.

Even though I have established logic and Marxist philosophy as foundational driving forces, I still found myself confused in practical application. Perhaps this is because the process of applying these theories in practice often brings new insights.

I have questioned how logic and philosophy can be applied in real life. Rui Xiaodan’s question, “What’s the use?” highlights this concern.

Useless might seem to have no direct application, but Uselessness aligns with a belief I have long held, although I had not fully committed to it before.

The boundary of Uselessness lies in the fact that logic and philosophy can indeed be applied to various aspects of daily life and work.

I feel fortunate to have gained some insight in my 30s. This insight has provided a clear answer from the “Dao”. The answer has been within me all along, only needing a reason to believe and persist.

An interesting question is: Humans often seek meaning in their actions, but is this meaning truly important?

I’m still not sure, but for many things, it’s crucial for me to understand “why”.

The concept of “Useless and Uselessness” embodies the principle of simplicity. What seems simple is actually challenging to achieve, while complex things can be simplified, often due to our misunderstanding or overcomplication of the unknown.

Laozi said: “Wu wei but nothing is not done.”

Following the “Dao”, seeking truth from facts, and observing the cause and effect in our actions.

What You Focus on Echoes Back

Rebirth

There’s a classic paradox in philosophy known as Theseus’ Ship. If the wooden planks of Theseus’ ship are gradually replaced until none of the original planks remain, is it still the same ship?

Some say yes, others say no. If our cells regenerate every day, and eventually, all of our cells are replaced, are we still the same person? And if our thoughts and perceptions evolve every day, are we still the same self?

Perhaps the answer doesn’t really matter. What matters is that life goes on, and new beginnings arise.

Material conditions shape consciousness, but consciousness also impacts material reality. A new understanding might lead to a form of rebirth.

In English, the prefix “re” signifies repetition or renewal, as in words like reborn, rework, rebuild, review, restart, and recall.

In the digital age, when new technological productivity emerges, every industry can be reinvented. From web1.0 to web2.0, and now the buzz around web3.0, each phase has led to the rebirth of various sectors.

For individuals, many of our perceptions are influenced by our upbringing and environment. However, these perceptions aren’t necessarily correct. Human behavior is driven by habits, and habits are shaped by consciousness. So, should we not re-examine our past knowledge, concepts, and beliefs?

This might be a long journey, but certain key ideas are worth re-evaluating. Over the past few years, I’ve been searching for the root of certain issues, and recently found answers in two books. Interestingly, both are textbooks: one is Formal Logic, and the other is Marxist Philosophy. Perhaps my curiosity and exposure to these subjects in college led me to this path of introspection.

In simple terms, it’s a re-evaluation of the Three Views: worldview, values, and outlook on life.

Marxist philosophy teaches that the relationship between humans and the world revolves around two main activities: understanding the world and transforming it. All human activities are centered on these two goals. This also explains why, in retrospect, “carving oneself and benefiting others” represents a fundamental drive and value. First, we must understand ourselves and the world, and then we transform it.

Everyone has the opportunity for a rebirth, but this opportunity requires a re-examination of oneself and the world—a baptism of the Three Views.

In my value system, only through a deep understanding of oneself and the world can we find a peaceful path forward.

The Process of Thinking Involves the Use of Concepts, Judgments, and Reasoning

Posted on 2022-04-30 Edited on 2024-08-15 In Times
Symbols count in article: 626 Reading time ≈ 2 mins.

We often talk about logic, but what exactly is logic?

The word “logic” is derived from the Greek word λσγοS (logos), originally meaning thought, reason, principle, word, etc. In everyday language, “logic” can have three different meanings:

First, it refers to the regularity of the interconnection and development of objective things, as well as the regularity of human thinking that reflects this regularity. For example, “the logic of the Chinese revolution” or “the logic of things.” Here, “logic” refers to the regularity of objective things. Similarly, “to draw a logical conclusion” or “the article has strong logic” refers to the regularity of thinking.

Second, it represents a particular theory, viewpoint, or method of looking at things. For example, invaders often label their aggression as “friendship,” which we denounce as “pure robber logic.” In this context, “logic” refers to the invaders’ twisted theory, viewpoint, or method of reasoning.

Third, it refers to the science that studies the forms and laws of correct thinking, i.e., formal logic and dialectical logic, though it is commonly used to refer to formal logic. For instance, “learn some grammar and logic” or “high school students should study logic.” Here, “logic” refers to the science of formal logic.

So, what is “formal logic”?

Formal logic studies the forms and laws of thinking and is, first and foremost, a science that studies thinking. Engels said that logic is the doctrine of the laws of the thinking process itself.

And what is “thinking,” and how does human thinking take place?

Mao Zedong explained in his “On Practice”: People initially see only the phenomena of various things in the process, the one-sidedness of each thing, and the external connections between things… This belongs to the perceptual stage of human cognition. At this stage, “people cannot yet form profound concepts or draw logical conclusions.” However, as social practice continues, the things that arouse sensations and impressions in people are repeatedly experienced, leading to a sudden transformation (a leap) in the cognitive process, resulting in the formation of concepts.

Recently, I was also unclear about the origin of “concepts,” and this passage explains it well.

The process of thinking is an abstract, indirect reflection of the objective world, characterized by abstraction and indirectness. This reflection is realized through language.

The thinking process involves the use of concepts, judgments, and reasoning, making these the essential forms of thinking used to reflect objective reality, which logic refers to as the forms of thought.

———

Most of the content above is derived from the book Formal Logic, compiled by the Department of Philosophy at East China Normal University. The book has reached its fifth edition. I’ve read the first edition, which was initially drafted in 1963. The fifth edition has improved significantly compared to the first.

Why am I interested in this book? The main reason is that I have always been contemplating how to correctly understand the world.

Formal logic focuses on the formal laws of thinking, without concern for the content itself. This aligns with what I’ve been pondering—is there a fixed paradigm for the reflection of the objective world to the subjective world? To me, logic is the underlying layer, consisting of three elements: concepts, judgments, and reasoning.

Science may not always be right, but it is, at least for now, the worldview I am committed to.

Sometimes I think that the education system in China is really lacking in the widespread teaching of logic. After becoming certain of this path, I might even become a preacher of logic someday.

In a sense, correctly understanding the world means understanding the dialectical relationship between me and the world—a spiraling, reciprocating path from the this-worldly to the otherworldly, a path of mutual interaction between consciousness and matter.

Habit is a Behavior, and Behavior is Easier to Change

Posted on 2022-04-29 Edited on 2024-08-15 In Times
Symbols count in article: 300 Reading time ≈ 1 mins.

A few days ago, I came across a short psychology video on Douyin from “This Thought Official” titled “What Should You Never Say to Your Child?” It was quite insightful.

Guest Huang Shiming answered: Negative language at the identity level should not be used.

For example, if a client says to me, “Hey, teacher, I’m an alcoholic,” and seeks psychological counseling.

I won’t agree with that. I would say, “Hey, are you saying that you have a habit of drinking?”

Habit is a behavior, and behavior is easier to change.

If someone says, “I’m a depressed person.”

I would respond, “Are you saying you smile a little less than others?”

If my child says, “Dad, I’m sure I’ll fail. I identify as someone who can’t succeed.”

I would say, “Oh, are you saying that this time, you might fail?”

This relaxes the tension (deep breath) and opens up a space for relaxation, where new things can come in.

Of course, they might still fail the exam tomorrow, but now we share an alliance, a common intention, to see how things can get a little better than they are now. I become that ally in my child’s eyes, the person they believe in.

———

Many times, whether labeling ourselves or others, it’s easy to apply definitive labels, such as “I’m a loser,” or “He’s a bad person.”

Carrying such a definitive label can be an invisible burden for years, sometimes suffocating. If you’re lucky, you might overcome it, but if not, it can become a vicious cycle.

Life has no boundaries, there aren’t as many labels as we think, and things shouldn’t be so easily defined. The world isn’t just black and white, good or bad. Often, time is the best companion, and a positive mindset can change a lot.

I Think, Therefore I Am

Posted on 2022-04-23 Edited on 2024-08-15 In Times
Symbols count in article: 499 Reading time ≈ 2 mins.

Descartes once said, “I think, therefore I am.”

Many years ago, I asked a question on Zhihu about how to understand Hegel’s phrase “What is real is rational.” For years, it seems I’ve been chasing the question: If my existence is rational, then why do I exist, and what makes my existence different?

Life, at times, seems to be a series of hypotheses—some societal, some personal. What follows is an endless pursuit and exploration surrounding these assumptions.

Lately, I’ve been reading two books: The Essence and The Power of Thinking. My interest in the concept of “essence” sparked this journey. Most of us seek to understand the essence of things, but it’s often elusive. So why can some people perceive the essence, while others cannot?

Most people recognize the importance of thinking—independent thinking, critical thinking, deep thinking, and reverse thinking. Yet, despite various thought processes being distilled over time, humanity has never fully explained the act of thinking itself, such as what consciousness is.

The Power of Thinking offers this definition of thinking: Thinking refers to the cognitive process of analyzing and judging one or more external objects to arrive at specific conclusions about them.

One question I’ve pondered for a long time is, “How can we understand the world?”

It’s a process that reflects the world onto the individual, moving from the objective world to the subjective, and from this shore to the other shore. It’s challenging but worth a lifetime of thought.

What you see in the world may not be true. It requires continuous verification to determine what is real. Some find this pursuit dull, but I find it deeply meaningful.

Biologically, thinking is essentially the result of nerve impulses between neurons, leading to reflexes and other behaviors.

We think every day, passively as information floods in, actively when faced with new problems, and sometimes unconsciously, even when our minds seem to wander.

But how should we think? How can we make our thinking more efficient? How can we turn our thoughts into actions?

While thinking may have no end, it can have dimensions—depth, breadth, boundaries, and stages. Thinking can even become reflexive, where we think about thinking itself—meta-thinking.

Sometimes understanding the rules of the world is simple, just a short journey from the object itself to the brain’s specific response. Yet this brief path is fraught with challenges, and one misstep could lead you astray, away from your intended destination.

In my view, human civilization persists because it is the crystallization of continuous thought and exploration by those who came before us. Sometimes I wonder, when you fully understand the thought processes of past greats, is that person who thinks just like them still you?

Schopenhauer said, “The man who thinks stands as the sovereign of his own spiritual kingdom.” May every thoughtful person reign over their own world and become the ruler of their own life.

Supporting Role or Leading Role?

Posted on 2022-04-20 Edited on 2024-08-15 In Times
Symbols count in article: 607 Reading time ≈ 2 mins.

Life is essentially a journey of playing different roles.

Many people’s first experience of transformation is the sudden realization that, though they thought they were the main character in their story, they were actually just a supporting role, or even merely a passerby.

As a child, I used to enjoy watching the main characters in movies. But as I grew older, I found myself more interested in the supporting roles, learning about the growth experiences of the smaller characters.

If life were a play, and the director only gave you 10 seconds, how would you seize that moment?

We play many roles throughout our lives, but often, we don’t fully understand these roles. Some roles seem to be handed to us unexpectedly.

We are born as sons or daughters, we go to school and become students, then we work and become employees, eventually rising to executive roles. We fall in love, get married, and become lovers, then eventually grandparents or take on many other roles.

But do we truly understand the meaning and responsibility behind each role?

One of my deepest reflections on roles came when I was first promoted to director. I was excited about the promotion and the raise, but when I started working in that role, managing both up and down the chain was incredibly challenging. For the first few months, I struggled, trying to handle everything personally, unable to prioritize effectively. My boss criticized me, my subordinates pushed back, and my team was in disarray.

Then one day, I suddenly understood what being a “director” really meant. I grasped the nature of the role, set clear boundaries, and focused on the essential tasks. Slowly, things started to improve. If you can’t quickly adapt to a new role, both you and your colleagues will feel exhausted.

What does it mean to be an employee? What does it mean to be a manager?

A true manager is someone who is willing to think proactively, take responsibility, guide others, and take action, rather than just giving orders. How many of your skills as a manager could actually surpass those of the specialists on the front line?

In life, what does it mean to be a child or a parent? A student or a teacher? A friend or a lover? Where are the boundaries of these roles, and what do we expect from the other person in each relationship?

Too often, we confuse our roles or lack clarity in them, or we passively fall into a role without taking it on actively.

Recently, there was a viral short video about a war game played abroad. The red and blue teams were battling for territory, and one side was retreating steadily. In the end, their commander logged off and fled. At that moment, a Chinese player stepped up and took over the command, leading the team to victory with bilingual English-Chinese instructions.

This Chinese player may have started the game simply wanting to play as a soldier, but when the moment came, they took on the role of commander and performed it perfectly.

The key here is “attentiveness” in daily life—paying attention, thinking carefully, daring to try and take responsibility. Only then can you rise to the occasion when it counts.

Many roles in life are assigned to us. If that’s the case, then let’s play those roles well. If one day the director gives you a 10-second role, will you seize it?

Wong Jing once remarked that Stephen Chow showed seven different expressions in a 10-second scene in A Chinese Odyssey. How much effort do you think went into perfecting those seven expressions?

I Am What I Am, a Firework of a Different Color

Posted on 2022-04-16 Edited on 2024-08-15 In Times
Symbols count in article: 562 Reading time ≈ 2 mins.

Formal Logic states that a concept is a form of thought that reflects the “unique” attributes (inherent or essential properties) of an object.

Recently, I’ve been exploring a question: Is there a way for personal breakthrough?

Continuous learning, organizing notes, building knowledge systems, cognitive awakening, and so on. Yet, I still feel unsettled, as if daily problems remain unsolved, always in doubt, always exploring.

In work communication between superiors and subordinates, it often seems like you’ve explained something clearly, but the final result differs significantly. When we reflect on it, we often find that there were discrepancies in how we understood certain concepts or things.

One day, while reading, I came across Einstein’s statement: “The most incomprehensible thing about the world is that it is comprehensible.” Having been puzzled by some issues for a long time, I was deeply moved by the word “comprehensible.”

Later, I realized that one way to understand the world is through “logic,” through induction and deduction, and the foundation of logic is “concepts.”

Thus, the paradigm for understanding the world, in my view, becomes quite simple: Have you truly understood a “concept”?

The most crucial aspect of a concept is its “characteristic,” the key factor that distinguishes it from other things. This understanding makes many problems much clearer.

The internet often mentions that if you can’t explain your product in one sentence, it’s not a qualified product. In business, this is about “positioning,” while in logic, it’s about “concept” description, a process of “definition.”

Many people say they can’t express themselves well. The reason for this is often that they haven’t thought things through; it’s a mess. The reason for this mess is unclear logic, and unclear logic is likely due to unclear “concepts.”

I now enjoy a new way of note-taking with Flomo: just recording “keywords.” This method is quick and efficient. On one hand, it provides a clue and facilitates indexing; on the other hand, it helps practice two logical abilities: “deduction” and “abstraction.” Later, when I have time, I can elaborate on these concepts, possibly leading to a small blog post.

Deduction involves expanding on a concept, making it more understandable, defining it, or providing examples. Abstraction involves simplifying complex matters, extracting commonalities, and being as concise as possible, which is the foundation of structured expression.

At last year’s NoteMan Annual Conference, Teacher Ke Zhou specifically mentioned “conceptual thinking,” a way of thinking. I looked it up and found that Huawei’s Teacher Su Guanzhe also frequently discusses it.

Conceptual Thinking is the ability to identify the essential characteristics of seemingly unrelated things.

In this sense, my understanding is that it is a thinking ability that sees the essence through phenomena. But it seems I haven’t explained it very clearly because there are many ways to see the essence through phenomena. This isn’t the unique trait of “conceptual thinking.” I’ve asked Teacher Ke Zhou some questions about this, and I’ll update when I receive new answers.

Personally, I believe that trying to understand the world is about understanding the concepts of all things. Understanding concepts is understanding the world.

Leslie Cheung sings, “I am what I am, a firework of a different color.”

Life Thrives on Negative Entropy Embrace the Pain

Posted on 2022-04-15 Edited on 2024-08-15 In Times
Symbols count in article: 152 Reading time ≈ 1 mins.

Pain is the sensation of suffering or a profound emotional impact.

Growth is a process of continually stepping out of your comfort zone. Staying in the comfort zone for too long can lead to a loss of critical thinking, an avoidance of reality, and a lack of crisis awareness.

Schrödinger once said, “Living is fighting against the increase of entropy; life thrives on negative entropy.”

This is one of my favorite quotes—both pessimistic and romantic.

Embracing pain is a form of reverence for life. Life itself is about “living towards death,” and life inherently embodies “impermanence.” Only by truly understanding these concepts can we unleash a powerful force for living.

Watching movies, listening to music, reading, exercising—there are many ways to keep both the mind and body in a state of “heartbeat.”

Keep the pain, keep the insights, live towards the light, and the future will only become more brilliant.
