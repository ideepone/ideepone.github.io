Thinking Model 2: The Theory Clock

Posted on 2024-09-04 In Models Word count in article: 446 Reading time ≈ 2 mins.

The Theory Clock: The Core of Cognition

In the realm of thinking models, The Theory Clock is a crucial concept that helps us understand the essence of cognition. This model primarily addresses two questions: What is a problem? And what standards should a good model meet?

Core Insights of The Theory Clock

The Theory Clock emphasizes that high cognition has the following standards:

  1. Seeing Far: A person with high cognition can foresee long-term trends, not just short-term phenomena.
  2. Seeing the Whole: They can understand the situation comprehensively but may not be able to predict every detail.
  3. Not Precise: Even if they can see far and wide, they cannot accurately predict the exact timing or details of specific events.

This viewpoint reflects the non-intuitive nature of cognition. Many people think that the measure of high cognition is whether one can accurately predict the future, but in reality, the core of cognition is grasping future trends rather than specific details.

Characteristics of a Model

A good cognitive model should have the following three key characteristics:

  1. Generality: The model should be widely applicable. For example, Newton’s Third Law is a theory with broad applicability.
  2. Simplicity: The model should be as simple as possible to understand and apply. For instance, focusing only on whether something is “expensive” or “cheap” ignores other factors.
  3. Accuracy: Accuracy in a model is often at odds with generality and simplicity. A model that aims for both generality and simplicity is usually less precise in specific details.

The Theory Clock and Upward Management

In practical applications, such as upward management (i.e., communicating with your boss), The Theory Clock helps us understand how to adjust communication strategies. For instance, if your initial communication with your boss does not succeed, you should continue to adjust your approach and try more times rather than simply dismissing the model or giving up. The high cognition attitude is to practice and adjust continuously, rather than seeking a one-size-fits-all solution.

Practice and the Practice Mindset

In practice, facing tasks or challenges, the practice mindset is crucial. A person with a practice mindset continuously tries and adjusts rather than relying on a single success or failure. The key to high cognition is to improve one’s ability through ongoing practice and optimization, rather than expecting to solve all problems easily with a particular model.

Summary

The Theory Clock reminds us that high cognition is not just about precise detail but about understanding trends deeply and comprehensively. An effective model should possess generality and simplicity, while individuals need to continuously adjust and optimize in practice to enhance the effectiveness of real-world applications.

Thinking Model 1: Cognitive-Action-Result

Posted on 2024-09-03 In Models Word count in article: 606 Reading time ≈ 2 mins.

Overview of the Cognitive-Action-Result Thinking Model

In this article, we introduce the first thinking model framework: the Cognitive-Action-Result Model. What does the Cognitive-Action-Result Model mean? It highlights a common issue where people focus on the results without considering that achieving these results requires actions. Results don’t just appear; they are the outcome of actions.

Many so-called “lazy questions” arise not from a lack of desire for results but from a reluctance to take necessary actions. People often seek others to do the work for them, demonstrating a lack of willingness to contribute effort. These individuals are effectively making requests or demands—they want the answer and expect someone else to carry out the actions. This attitude is largely ineffective in competitive environments, as no one is inclined to do the work for them.

Cognitive: Systematizing the Nature of Problems

So, how do we generate actions? First, you need to establish cognition. Understanding a problem is not just about surface-level observations but involves a systematic thinking framework. Cognition means viewing the essence of problems systematically. Systematization is a concept that will be discussed extensively in our course. Systematic cognition helps us understand the essence of problems and guides our actions. Only by grasping the essence can our actions be sustained.

People often ask how to persist. I frequently respond that it’s due to a lack of understanding of the problem’s essence, which makes it hard to persist. Without understanding the essence, actions do not receive positive feedback, leading to a lack of motivation. Systematic understanding of the essence enables continued action. Actions must be continually adjusted, with specific problems analyzed in detail to achieve expected results. Through continuous review of results, we further enhance our ability to view the essence systematically, forming a cycle of “Cognition-Action-Result.”

Action: The Path from Cognition to Practice

Remember, after learning this first model, focus not only on the results but on understanding the essence of the problem. Frequently asking “how to do it” or “what to do” addresses only the surface of the issue. The key is understanding the essence of the problem. Systematic cognition leads to effective actions and better results. If results are unsatisfactory, review and adjust your cognition to address any discrepancies in the essence and make improvements.

Result: Achieving Desired Outcomes through Action

In practice, improving cognition and using a systematic framework to understand problems can help you take more effective actions and achieve better results. By continuously adjusting and optimizing cognition and actions, you will be able to achieve your goals and enhance your performance in practical applications.

Case Analysis: Practical Application and Effects

Let’s examine some real-life examples. For instance, the cost difference between a 899 yuan career coaching program and a 40,000 yuan training course may seem significant, but from an economic perspective, what is considered “expensive”? If you are the end buyer, are you willing to pay this cost? If the 899 yuan training leads to significant career improvements and is covered by your company, its value is justified. Similarly, if the 40,000 yuan training course helps you gain more opportunities and benefits in your career, it is also justified.

Summary and Enhancement: Optimizing the Cognitive-Action-Result Model

Ultimately, analyzing problems from an economic perspective can help us better understand the concepts of “expensive” and “cheap.” When evaluating the value of anything, we should consider who bears the final cost and whether this cost aligns with a reasonable price definition. Using a systematic thinking framework allows us to more accurately assess and optimize the application of the Cognitive-Action-Result Model.

Why Do Humans Ask "Why"?

Posted on 2022-11-06 Edited on 2024-08-13 In Times Word count in article: 390 Reading time ≈ 1 mins.

Seeing mountains as mountains, and water as water;

Seeing mountains as not mountains, and water as not water;

Seeing mountains still as mountains, and water still as water.

At first, humans might not have asked “why.” But when did humans consciously start asking “why”? This is the origin.

Why can the 「2W1H」 framework help us think through certain problems, and why does this framework clarify some matters?

What is What, what is Why, and what is How?

Why are there three, and why are these three?

We are thinking, but we are rarely aware that we are thinking.

Where is the source of thought? In the brain.

Where is the source of the brain? In consciousness.

One of philosophy’s focuses might be on the origin.

If humans ask why, what might result? They might obtain an effect.

If humans don’t ask why, what might happen? They might just follow a predetermined path.

We continuously strive to explore the origin of this world, constantly asking why in order to understand the world clearly. We are all exploring the rationality of existence. There is always something implicit behind this: 「causal logic.」

It’s difficult for us to explain how the brain expressed and understood the world before consciousness, how children understand the world, how they think, or if they even think at all. Is our thinking as adults a kind of imprisoned thinking?

This world seems to have a “cause” for everything. The moment humans ask a question, there might be an unalterable belief within consciousness: belief in “causality.”

I still vividly remember a scene from “Divine Providence” where Han Xin tries to understand and believe in the existence of aliens. Later, in “New Song,” I was amazed by Wang Anshi’s awareness that even if someone knew the future, it wouldn’t be enough to change anything.

A possible law of this world is that 「everything has a cause and effect.」

But, is that true?

Does an absolute belief in causality prevent you from truly understanding the root of the world? Just as what we call absolute might just be a special case of a probabilistic event, this world might be primarily composed of dark matter, with matter being only a part of it.

When what we firmly believe to be the essence is merely a facade, will you still believe in this world?

Useless and Uselessness

Posted on 2022-05-15 Edited on 2024-08-23 In Times Word count in article: 444 Reading time ≈ 2 mins.

“Dao”

This weekend, I revisited the TV series The Dao and came across a profound discussion about the concept of “Dao”:

———

Ding Yuanying said: “There are three levels to understanding society: technology, systems, and culture. Whether for an individual or a nation, any fate ultimately stems from cultural attributes. Dominant cultures create strong individuals, while weak cultures produce weak ones. This is a rule, and it can be understood as **the Dao, which is not subject to human will.**”

“Dominant cultures follow the laws of things, while weak cultures rely on the moral expectations of the strong, seeking salvation. Dominant cultures are like ‘secret manuals’ in martial arts, while weak cultures, being easier to learn, understand, and use, become popular.”

Rui Xiaodan asked: “I can understand this from the literal sense, but how does it apply?”

Ding Yuanying replied: “**Useless and Uselessness.**”

———

The phrase “Useless and Uselessness” precisely addresses the answer to my recent intellectual dilemma.

The term “Dao” represents the principles I have long sought, things that are not subject to personal will, which could be laws, ways of thinking, or various forms.

Through prolonged exploration, I have come to believe in the foundational role of Formal Logic and the worldview of Marxist Philosophy.

Even though I have established logic and Marxist philosophy as foundational driving forces, I still found myself confused in practical application. Perhaps this is because the process of applying these theories in practice often brings new insights.

I have questioned how logic and philosophy can be applied in real life. Rui Xiaodan’s question, “What’s the use?” highlights this concern.

Useless might seem to have no direct application, but Uselessness aligns with a belief I have long held, although I had not fully committed to it before.

The boundary of Uselessness lies in the fact that logic and philosophy can indeed be applied to various aspects of daily life and work.

I feel fortunate to have gained some insight in my 30s. This insight has provided a clear answer from the “Dao”. The answer has been within me all along, only needing a reason to believe and persist.

An interesting question is: Humans often seek meaning in their actions, but is this meaning truly important?

I’m still not sure, but for many things, it’s crucial for me to understand “why”.

The concept of “Useless and Uselessness” embodies the principle of simplicity. What seems simple is actually challenging to achieve, while complex things can be simplified, often due to our misunderstanding or overcomplication of the unknown.

Laozi said: “Wu wei but nothing is not done.”

Following the “Dao”, seeking truth from facts, and observing the cause and effect in our actions.

I Think, Therefore I Am

Posted on 2022-04-23 Edited on 2024-08-15 In Times Word count in article: 499 Reading time ≈ 2 mins.

Descartes once said, “I think, therefore I am.”

Many years ago, I asked a question on Zhihu about how to understand Hegel’s phrase “What is real is rational.” For years, it seems I’ve been chasing the question: If my existence is rational, then why do I exist, and what makes my existence different?

Life, at times, seems to be a series of hypotheses—some societal, some personal. What follows is an endless pursuit and exploration surrounding these assumptions.

Lately, I’ve been reading two books: The Essence and The Power of Thinking. My interest in the concept of “essence” sparked this journey. Most of us seek to understand the essence of things, but it’s often elusive. So why can some people perceive the essence, while others cannot?

Most people recognize the importance of thinking—independent thinking, critical thinking, deep thinking, and reverse thinking. Yet, despite various thought processes being distilled over time, humanity has never fully explained the act of thinking itself, such as what consciousness is.

The Power of Thinking offers this definition of thinking: Thinking refers to the cognitive process of analyzing and judging one or more external objects to arrive at specific conclusions about them.

One question I’ve pondered for a long time is, “How can we understand the world?”

It’s a process that reflects the world onto the individual, moving from the objective world to the subjective, and from this shore to the other shore. It’s challenging but worth a lifetime of thought.

What you see in the world may not be true. It requires continuous verification to determine what is real. Some find this pursuit dull, but I find it deeply meaningful.

Biologically, thinking is essentially the result of nerve impulses between neurons, leading to reflexes and other behaviors.

We think every day, passively as information floods in, actively when faced with new problems, and sometimes unconsciously, even when our minds seem to wander.

But how should we think? How can we make our thinking more efficient? How can we turn our thoughts into actions?

While thinking may have no end, it can have dimensions—depth, breadth, boundaries, and stages. Thinking can even become reflexive, where we think about thinking itself—meta-thinking.

Sometimes understanding the rules of the world is simple, just a short journey from the object itself to the brain’s specific response. Yet this brief path is fraught with challenges, and one misstep could lead you astray, away from your intended destination.

In my view, human civilization persists because it is the crystallization of continuous thought and exploration by those who came before us. Sometimes I wonder, when you fully understand the thought processes of past greats, is that person who thinks just like them still you?

Schopenhauer said, “The man who thinks stands as the sovereign of his own spiritual kingdom.” May every thoughtful person reign over their own world and become the ruler of their own life.
