Suddenly Looking Back, That Person Is at the Lantern Festival

Posted on 2022-04-04 Edited on 2024-08-23 In Times Word count in article: 472 Reading time ≈ 2 mins.

“State·Landscape”

Recently, discussions about “higher dimensions” often refer to an elevation in one’s state of mind.

One of my favorite Zen sayings is:

“Seeing a mountain is just seeing a mountain, seeing water is just seeing water;

Seeing a mountain is no longer just a mountain, seeing water is no longer just water;

Seeing a mountain is once again seeing a mountain, seeing water is once again seeing water.”

At various turning points in life, I like to reflect on this saying. When I first read it during my school years, I understood it, but gradually realized that I only grasped it on a superficial level. After living for half my life and experiencing many things, I found that much of the wisdom behind it comes from “experience.”

When new things appear, our first reaction is often to see only the surface—this is a mountain, this is water. Curiosity might drive us to explore, or these things might simply intrude into our lives.

When you delve deeper into the characteristics of these things, you might discover they differ from your initial perception.

A good example from life: We have learned so many Chinese characters since elementary school. While recognizing characters in paragraphs and sentences is straightforward, seeing a single character in isolation might suddenly feel unfamiliar, even leading to doubts about whether it’s written correctly.

Many aspects of life and work are similar. What seems like a simple job might become more complex when actually undertaken. Can you do it well? Can you maintain it year after year, day after day? When time is added into the equation, things might become less straightforward, and you may find yourself re-evaluating them.

When you start to seriously address a matter; when you analyze it thoroughly from beginning to end, from inside to out; and even when you take it on as a lifelong pursuit, you will become increasingly at ease.

Mr. Wang Guowei also summarized the three realms of life:

  1. Last night’s west wind withered the green tree; climbing the high tower alone, gazing at the road to the horizon.
  2. The belt grows wider, yet no regret; I have withered away for her.
  3. Searching for him a thousand times in the crowd, suddenly looking back, that person is at the lantern festival.

In Chinese discussions of states, people often say that they are only to be felt and not explained. This may be because the experience is personal, and only one’s own experience can reveal its true essence.

The Zen Master Huineng said:

“Without a Bodhi tree, and the bright mirror is not a platform;

Originally, there is nothing, where can dust cling?”

Perhaps after you have traveled through landscapes, looking back, you will find that it is all but a gentle cloud, which is the essence of completeness.
