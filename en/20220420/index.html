Supporting Role or Leading Role?

Posted on 2022-04-20 Edited on 2024-08-15 In Times
Symbols count in article: 607 Reading time ≈ 2 mins.

Life is essentially a journey of playing different roles.

Many people’s first experience of transformation is the sudden realization that, though they thought they were the main character in their story, they were actually just a supporting role, or even merely a passerby.

As a child, I used to enjoy watching the main characters in movies. But as I grew older, I found myself more interested in the supporting roles, learning about the growth experiences of the smaller characters.

If life were a play, and the director only gave you 10 seconds, how would you seize that moment?

We play many roles throughout our lives, but often, we don’t fully understand these roles. Some roles seem to be handed to us unexpectedly.

We are born as sons or daughters, we go to school and become students, then we work and become employees, eventually rising to executive roles. We fall in love, get married, and become lovers, then eventually grandparents or take on many other roles.

But do we truly understand the meaning and responsibility behind each role?

One of my deepest reflections on roles came when I was first promoted to director. I was excited about the promotion and the raise, but when I started working in that role, managing both up and down the chain was incredibly challenging. For the first few months, I struggled, trying to handle everything personally, unable to prioritize effectively. My boss criticized me, my subordinates pushed back, and my team was in disarray.

Then one day, I suddenly understood what being a “director” really meant. I grasped the nature of the role, set clear boundaries, and focused on the essential tasks. Slowly, things started to improve. If you can’t quickly adapt to a new role, both you and your colleagues will feel exhausted.

What does it mean to be an employee? What does it mean to be a manager?

A true manager is someone who is willing to think proactively, take responsibility, guide others, and take action, rather than just giving orders. How many of your skills as a manager could actually surpass those of the specialists on the front line?

In life, what does it mean to be a child or a parent? A student or a teacher? A friend or a lover? Where are the boundaries of these roles, and what do we expect from the other person in each relationship?

Too often, we confuse our roles or lack clarity in them, or we passively fall into a role without taking it on actively.

Recently, there was a viral short video about a war game played abroad. The red and blue teams were battling for territory, and one side was retreating steadily. In the end, their commander logged off and fled. At that moment, a Chinese player stepped up and took over the command, leading the team to victory with bilingual English-Chinese instructions.

This Chinese player may have started the game simply wanting to play as a soldier, but when the moment came, they took on the role of commander and performed it perfectly.

The key here is “attentiveness” in daily life—paying attention, thinking carefully, daring to try and take responsibility. Only then can you rise to the occasion when it counts.

Many roles in life are assigned to us. If that’s the case, then let’s play those roles well. If one day the director gives you a 10-second role, will you seize it?

Wong Jing once remarked that Stephen Chow showed seven different expressions in a 10-second scene in A Chinese Odyssey. How much effort do you think went into perfecting those seven expressions?
