The Essence of Education

Posted on 2022-04-01 Edited on 2024-08-15 In Times
Symbols count in article: 557 Reading time ≈ 2 mins.

A few days ago, I came across a short video discussing “What is the essence of education?” I found it quite insightful, so I’d like to share it:

———

When Michelangelo completed the statue of David, he was astounded, and so was everyone else.

A journalist asked Michelangelo, “How did you create such a magnificent work?”

He didn’t answer immediately. Instead, he pondered for a long time before carefully responding:

“It wasn’t I who created this great work; David was already inside the stone. I simply set him free.”

These words are brilliant, capturing the timeless essence of education.

“Every person has a better self, perhaps even a great self, within them. Every child undoubtedly possesses such a self.”

———

When it comes to education, I believe it’s important to consider two roles: the educator and the learner.

For educators—whether they are teachers or elders—I appreciate the approach of encouragement and discovery. Just like in the video, the idea that “David” was already within the stone suggests that educators should first believe that they are merely guides, playing a role in passing on knowledge. The future will belong to the younger generation, as this is ingrained in our species’ genetics.

Many educators prefer to impose authority and suppress with their status, which is not ideal. Young people have their own consciousness and think about issues from different perspectives. We should communicate as equals, discussing the logic of the matter itself.

Educators are educators primarily because they have taken steps ahead, possessing more experience, or perhaps they have stronger or more specialized skills. However, these “experiences” or “skills” are not necessarily correct and should be open to discussion. It is through this process that educators hope learners will ultimately acquire these experiences to create more value.

For learners, the most crucial element is “confidence”—to believe that they are unique and excellent. Of course, confidence should not be blind or arrogant; it should be built on continuous self-awareness and learning, grounded in a scientific and dialectical attitude.

I believe two insights are particularly important:

  1. Success is built on the improvement gained through multiple failures and requires deliberate practice.

The more valuable something is, the more effort and resistance it usually involves. I’ve always believed that things easily obtained in this world are rare; occasional success might come from sheer luck, but one cannot rely entirely on such rare events. Instead, it’s essential to continually experience and try, to enhance one’s comprehensive abilities across multiple fields, and to strengthen the capacity for survival and growth. This ability requires constant learning and adaptation—lifelong learning for lifelong growth.

  1. Positive psychological feedback from oneself and the environment, such as praise and encouragement.

Often, success is not guaranteed but possible. However, the subconscious influence of positive psychology can sometimes help one persevere, and persistence often leads to success. Nevertheless, it’s also important to consider “sunk costs.” If too much time and effort have been invested in something without success, it might be worth reconsidering further investment—a common mistake in entrepreneurship.

Both educators and learners should first gain a clearer understanding of themselves and then of others and the organizations they are part of. We must discover our true selves and learn to unearth the “true self” in others, so that human civilization can continue to be passed down.
