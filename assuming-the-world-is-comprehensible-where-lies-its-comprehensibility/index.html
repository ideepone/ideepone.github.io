Assuming the World Is Comprehensible, Where Lies Its Comprehensibility?

Posted on 2023-01-06 Edited on 2024-08-26 In Times Word count in article: 408 Reading time ≈ 1 mins.
Exploring the nature of comprehension in a probabilistic world and the distinction between randomness and uncertainty.

Comprehension

The most incomprehensible thing about the world is that it is comprehensible.
— Albert Einstein

Albert Einstein’s insight into 「comprehension」 and its applicability to the world prompts us to ask: What exactly did he mean?

Was his perspective rooted in quantum mechanics, probabilistic randomness, or something else? Although the exact origins of this quote are debated, its core question remains: Where does the 「domain」 of “comprehension” lie?

Einstein famously said, “God does not play dice.”

However, the behavior of microscopic particles suggests that randomness and probability are intrinsic to our world. This randomness extends to the macroscopic world, where countless decisions and actions are influenced by complex factors and conditions.

Socrates

All men are mortal;
Socrates is a man;
Therefore, Socrates is mortal.

Consider this: What if probability were integrated into inductive and deductive logic?

99% of all men are mortal;
Socrates has an 80% chance of being a man;
What is the likelihood of Socrates being mortal?

Randomness vs. Uncertainty

The distinction between randomness and uncertainty is crucial:

Randomness means that while the possible outcomes of an event are known, the specific outcome remains unpredictable.

Previously, I thought of this as process thinking—believing that understanding the entire process or having a divine perspective would make things appear natural.

However, reality often defies this view. For example, why do diverse ideologies and groups exist? Even the existence of Earth might seem random.

Our efforts to understand the future involve transforming uncertainties into randomness. Most aspects of the world are random rather than uncertain.

God might enjoy playing dice, but knowing that the dice only have numbers 1 through 6 is a different matter from not knowing if the dice could have numbers beyond 6. The latter represents uncertainty, which can be unsettling.

The Endpoint of Probability Is 1

Probability has three key properties:

  1. Probability ranges between 0 and 1; it cannot be negative.
  2. The sum of the probabilities of all basic events in the sample space is 1.
  3. The probability of an event not occurring is 1 minus the probability of it occurring.

The second property is particularly intriguing: the sum of probabilities equals 1, or 100%. This “1” signifies two things:

  1. The sample must be complete and without omissions, a challenging task in reality.
  2. Probability represents a necessary event within a system, but it is contingent upon specific details.

To maximize the likelihood of an event, one must thoroughly understand it.
