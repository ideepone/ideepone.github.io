A Sudden Turn, and That Person Was There in the Faint Light

发表于 2022-04-04 更新于 2024-08-15 分类于 Times
本文字数： 474 阅读时长 ≈ 2 分钟

Recently, discussions about “dimension ascension” have centered around the idea of elevating one’s state of being.

There’s a Zen saying I deeply appreciate:

“At first, a mountain is a mountain, and water is water;
Then, a mountain is no longer a mountain, and water is no longer water;
Finally, a mountain is just a mountain, and water is just water.”

I often reflect on this phrase at various turning points in my life. During my student years, I understood it, but over time, I realized that my understanding was superficial. As I’ve lived and experienced more, I’ve come to see that the true essence of this wisdom lies in “experience.”

When something new appears in life, the first reaction is to see only the surface—this is a mountain, this is water. Perhaps curiosity drives us to explore further, or perhaps these things simply intrude upon our lives.

As you delve deeper into the nature of things, you might start to see that they aren’t quite what you initially thought.

There’s a relatable example in everyday life: After learning so many Chinese characters, we can easily recognize them in sentences and paragraphs. Yet, when we isolate a single character, it may suddenly appear unfamiliar, making us question whether we’ve written it correctly.

Life and work often mirror this process. At first, a job may seem like just a job. But when you’re asked to do it, can you do it well? Can you maintain that level of performance year after year? Often, when you add the dimension of time, these tasks become more challenging, and you might find yourself re-evaluating them.

When you start to take something seriously again—when you begin to analyze it deeply from start to finish, inside and out—when you commit to something as a lifelong pursuit, you will find a growing sense of calm.

Wang Guowei summarized the three realms of life:

  1. The west wind withered the green trees last night, I ascend the lone tower, gazing far down the endless road.

  2. My belt is getting looser, yet I do not regret it, for I have grown haggard in the pursuit of you.

  3. I sought him a thousand times in the crowd, and suddenly turned, there he was, in the dim light.

When Chinese people discuss realms or states of being, they often emphasize that such things can only be understood through experience, not words, because the feelings are personal and can only be truly grasped by oneself.

As the Zen master Hui Neng once said:

“Bodhi originally has no tree, the bright mirror has no stand;
Originally there is not a single thing, so where can dust alight?”

Perhaps, after wandering through mountains and waters, when you look back and see nothing but serene clouds and gentle winds, you will have reached fulfillment.
