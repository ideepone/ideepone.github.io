I Think, Therefore I Am

Posted on 2022-04-23 Edited on 2024-08-15 In Times Word count in article: 499 Reading time ≈ 2 mins.

Descartes once said, “I think, therefore I am.”

Many years ago, I asked a question on Zhihu about how to understand Hegel’s phrase “What is real is rational.” For years, it seems I’ve been chasing the question: If my existence is rational, then why do I exist, and what makes my existence different?

Life, at times, seems to be a series of hypotheses—some societal, some personal. What follows is an endless pursuit and exploration surrounding these assumptions.

Lately, I’ve been reading two books: The Essence and The Power of Thinking. My interest in the concept of “essence” sparked this journey. Most of us seek to understand the essence of things, but it’s often elusive. So why can some people perceive the essence, while others cannot?

Most people recognize the importance of thinking—independent thinking, critical thinking, deep thinking, and reverse thinking. Yet, despite various thought processes being distilled over time, humanity has never fully explained the act of thinking itself, such as what consciousness is.

The Power of Thinking offers this definition of thinking: Thinking refers to the cognitive process of analyzing and judging one or more external objects to arrive at specific conclusions about them.

One question I’ve pondered for a long time is, “How can we understand the world?”

It’s a process that reflects the world onto the individual, moving from the objective world to the subjective, and from this shore to the other shore. It’s challenging but worth a lifetime of thought.

What you see in the world may not be true. It requires continuous verification to determine what is real. Some find this pursuit dull, but I find it deeply meaningful.

Biologically, thinking is essentially the result of nerve impulses between neurons, leading to reflexes and other behaviors.

We think every day, passively as information floods in, actively when faced with new problems, and sometimes unconsciously, even when our minds seem to wander.

But how should we think? How can we make our thinking more efficient? How can we turn our thoughts into actions?

While thinking may have no end, it can have dimensions—depth, breadth, boundaries, and stages. Thinking can even become reflexive, where we think about thinking itself—meta-thinking.

Sometimes understanding the rules of the world is simple, just a short journey from the object itself to the brain’s specific response. Yet this brief path is fraught with challenges, and one misstep could lead you astray, away from your intended destination.

In my view, human civilization persists because it is the crystallization of continuous thought and exploration by those who came before us. Sometimes I wonder, when you fully understand the thought processes of past greats, is that person who thinks just like them still you?

Schopenhauer said, “The man who thinks stands as the sovereign of his own spiritual kingdom.” May every thoughtful person reign over their own world and become the ruler of their own life.
